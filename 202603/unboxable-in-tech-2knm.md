<p><em>This is a submission for the <a href="https://dev.to/challenges/wecoded-2026">2026 WeCoded Challenge</a>: Echoes of Experience</em></p>




<p><strong>"I don't know which box to put you in."</strong></p>

<p>I've been hearing those words for thirty years.</p>

<p>And every single time, it lands like a slap.</p>

<p>The violence isn't in the words. The violence is in what the words actually mean.</p>

<p>It's not: <em>which box could I put you in?</em></p>

<p>It's: <em>you make me uncomfortable. So I won't hire you.</em></p>

<p>In a lot of companies, recruitment works like that old Eastern European joke: you give two agents a round peg and a square hole. Two solutions: enlarge the hole… or hit the peg harder.</p>

<p>Guess what the industry has been choosing?</p>

<p>It hits. Harder. Again and again.</p>

<p>Tonight I'm not going to tell you a story about resilience.<br>
I'm going to show you the cost of refusing to fit the box.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  Exhibit 1 — The Unlikely Internship
</h2>

<p>End of studies. 1989.</p>

<p>Everyone is chasing SSII firms, banks, impressive CVs.</p>

<p>Me? I choose a middle school. A library. An 8088.</p>

<p>I know perfectly well it won't lead to a job.</p>

<p>That's not the point.</p>

<p>I want human contact. Real problems. Everyday life.</p>

<p>At a software firm, I would have coded screens. Batch procedures. Efficient within my lane. Never responsible for the whole.</p>

<p>Here, I build a complete application. From scratch. With real users in front of me. Immediate feedback.</p>

<p>I learn what no one would have taught me elsewhere: how to think an entire system.</p>

<p>Not just my part of the ticket.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  Exhibit 2 — The Printer
</h2>

<p>Architecture firm. 1993.</p>

<p>HP printer. Designed for PC and mainframe use. 132 columns, continuous paper feed. 36 switches on the back panel, combinable in non-linear ways.</p>

<p>It had never printed a single accent.</p>

<p>The firm called HP support.</p>

<p>And waited. For months.</p>

<p>Me? I call HP. I offer: "Do you want me to come to Grenoble to pick up the manual myself? I'm in Aveyron. 400 kilometres. I'm ready."</p>

<p>Three weeks later, the manual arrives. 300 pages. Pure English. ASCII only. Not a word about French. Not a word about single-sheet feeding.</p>

<p>I read all 300 pages. I understand the logic. I deduce. I test.</p>

<p>The firm prints in French. On A4. Perfectly.</p>

<p>An IT department would still be waiting for support today.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  Exhibit 3 — The Cleaning Company
</h2>

<p>A cleaning company. A simple need: manage their activity, schedules, clients.</p>

<p>They're advised to go with Windows 3.1 + Office.</p>

<p>The hype of the moment. Everyone's doing it, so they should too.</p>

<p>Me? I look at the actual need.</p>

<p>OS/2. Lotus Symphony.</p>

<p>Twice as cheap. Twice as stable. Perfectly suited to what they actually do.</p>

<p>And the price difference? It funded a 486DX40 instead of an SX25. 16MB of RAM instead of 4. A 240MB hard drive.</p>

<p>The entire market is turning its back on these tools? So be it.</p>

<p>The need comes before the trend. And the saved budget goes into the machine.</p>

<p>An IT department would have ordered the Microsoft licences and called it modernisation.</p>

<p>I delivered a system that held up — on a machine that could actually run it.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  Exhibit 4 — The SELECT COUNT(*)
</h2>

<p>Paris. 2010.</p>

<p>A project manager wants to catch me out in front of the team.</p>

<p>I write a <code>SELECT COUNT(*)</code>.</p>

<p>He jumps on it. "We don't do <code>SELECT *</code>."</p>

<p>I explain. For MySQL, <code>COUNT(*)</code> is the most performant solution. He tests.</p>

<p>He concedes.</p>

<p>But the tension remains.</p>

<p>Because I contradicted him. In front of everyone. When he had been trying to humiliate me.</p>

<p>I wasn't the one with a problem.</p>

<p>He was the one who couldn't stand being wrong.</p>

<p>In these companies, they don't want people who think.</p>

<p>They want people who validate.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  Exhibit 5 — The Open Space
</h2>

<p>In an open space, you don't model systems. You survive the noise.</p>

<p>You don't think. You manage interruptions.</p>

<p>The industry calls that collaboration.</p>

<p>I call it destroying the concentration of the people who need it to work.</p>

<p>I turned down assignments because of this.</p>

<p>A line I never crossed.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  Exhibit 6 — Logic-immo
</h2>

<p>One exception.</p>

<p>Just one in five years.</p>

<p>Logic-immo. 2008-2009.</p>

<p>Brought in for Zend Framework.</p>

<p>Left having designed and built an Oracle-to-MySQL data extraction system in PHP CLI.</p>

<p>Why?</p>

<p>Because someone had looked at what I could actually do.</p>

<p>And decided to give priority to the need.</p>

<p>Someone had read between the lines.</p>

<p>Rare. Worth mentioning.</p>

<p>In 2011, I was burned out.</p>

<p>In 2012, my daughter was born.</p>

<p>I left Paris.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  Exhibit 7 — The Regional Directory
</h2>

<p>Back in the South-West.</p>

<p>A client. A monstrous project: local directory, classifieds, events section for every town and village, dedicated site for every business.</p>

<p>Over a hundred professional sites to generate and manage dynamically. Custom routing. Zend Framework. Smarty.</p>

<p>A standard agency wouldn't have lasted three months.</p>

<p>Not because of the project.</p>

<p>Because of the client.</p>

<p>Hypochondriac. Always wound up. Always in crisis mode. Unmanageable for anyone without patience.</p>

<p>I held on. Ten months. Solo. Architecture built from zero.</p>

<p>To the point where I fantasised about a permanent contract.</p>

<p>Any one. Just to never have that kind of client again.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  Exhibit 8 — The B2B Platform
</h2>

<p>A matchmaking platform for business conventions.</p>

<p>The existing application took ten minutes to load.</p>

<p>Ten minutes.</p>

<p>Server ten years past its prime. First-generation Symfony. Poorly designed PostgreSQL database. Non-relational data. Queries going in every direction.</p>

<p>Nobody had asked the question: why is it slow?</p>

<p>I asked the question.</p>

<p>Custom framework inspired by ZF, but lighter. MySQL. Data schema rebuilt from scratch. Processes rewritten from the ground up. Fluid, responsive, usable interface. And a proper server.</p>

<p>Under thirty seconds.</p>

<p>That's not magic.</p>

<p>That's what happens when you look at the problem before touching the code.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  Exhibit 9 — The Projects Nobody Asked For
</h2>

<p>Nobody commissioned these.</p>

<p>They come from an impulse. A conversation. A problem I noticed.</p>

<p>Dev.to ranks me in the top 7 of the week. I could say thanks and move on.</p>

<p>Instead I implement RFC 2324.</p>

<p>The HTTP protocol for controlling a coffee maker. A 1998 joke buried in the Internet standards. Implemented seriously. Raw asyncio TCP server. Per-pot locks. Compliant headers.</p>

<p>In the comments, Sylwia asks when I'm going to implement a protocol for beer.</p>

<p>I run with it.</p>

<p>RFC 1516. The Hyper Text Beer Mug Control Protocol. Port 1414, a nod to Gdańsk.</p>

<p>The CV as a graph of relationships — because a profile like mine isn't linear. A timeline says nothing. A graph says everything.</p>

<p>AJC Bridge — write from WordPress, publish everywhere. Submitted to a challenge. Runner-up.</p>

<p>No ticket. No meeting. No box.</p>

<p>Just a problem, an impulse, and someone who gets on with it.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  Exhibit 10 — The Handover and the Veto
</h2>

<p>I coach a young entrepreneur. WordPress, Divi, Plesk.</p>

<p>I set up his servers. I pass on what I know.</p>

<p>And one simple principle: never put all your eggs in one basket. Always keep external backups.</p>

<p>OVH burns down.</p>

<p>He manages 60 sites. He loses them — theoretically.</p>

<p>He calls me.</p>

<p>48 hours later, everything is back up on new servers.</p>

<p>It wasn't me who saved his sites.</p>

<p>It was advice given months earlier.</p>

<p>We keep a great relationship.</p>

<p>He's part of a BNI. He puts my name forward for a training assignment.</p>

<p>Veto.</p>

<p>Not standardised.</p>

<p>The system would rather go without someone who just saved 60 sites in under 48 hours.</p>

<p>Think about that for a moment.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  Exhibit 11 — The Classroom Aide
</h2>

<p>I discovered this job over ten years ago.</p>

<p>My daughter's mother is a classroom aide for students with disabilities. She told me about it. I thought it was a job that made sense.</p>

<p>In 2021, a position opens near me. I jump at it.</p>

<p>The recruiter confirms in under five minutes. I barely have time to go down the stairs, step outside the building — he's already calling me back to come sign the contract.</p>

<p>For a while, it was exactly what I'd imagined. Students in difficulty. Concrete work. Visible results.</p>

<p>Some of the students I supported no longer need a classroom aide today.</p>

<p>That's the job.</p>

<p>Except the expectations have shifted.</p>

<p>Because there's no space in the specialist units. No space in the vocational streams. No space in the adapted classrooms.</p>

<p>So these students get placed in mainstream education. And a classroom aide gets put in front of them.</p>

<p>That's not educational support. That's the work of a specialist educator — paid twice as much, with the training and recognition that come with it.</p>

<p>I'm paid minimum wage. Half-time.</p>

<p>And some teachers have one request: shut up and stay in your lane.</p>

<p>The system does here exactly what it does everywhere else.</p>

<p>It puts the round peg in the square hole.</p>

<p>And when it doesn't fit, it hits harder.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  The Mechanism
</h2>

<p>WeCoded talks about marginalised voices in tech.</p>

<p>Marginalised. One word. Dozens of realities.</p>

<p>It's not only about gender, skin colour, sexual orientation.</p>

<p>Racism, homophobia, the rejection of an atypical career path, the rejection of the wrong kind of degree — all of it belongs to the same family.</p>

<p>The family of refusing the other.</p>

<p>The family of human stupidity organised into a system.</p>

<p>The mechanism is always the same.</p>

<p><em>You don't fit in the box. So you don't exist. **And you won't.</em>**</p>

<p>And that marginalisation hurts all the more because it makes no sense.</p>

<p>It can be justified — process, boxes, grids.</p>

<p>But every justification will itself be unjustifiable.</p>




<p>What I want you to take away is this:</p>

<h2>
  
  
  The Verdict
</h2>

<p>In all of these stories, one constant.</p>

<p>I always solved the problem I was called in for.</p>

<p>Always.</p>

<p>And yet I remain "unclassifiable". Not employable. Not standardised. Not in the box.</p>

<p>So the real question — the one that stings — is simple.</p>

<p>Who's the problem?</p>

<p>Me — the one who solves the problems?</p>

<p>Or the system — the one that doesn't know what to do with me between fires?</p>

<p>For thirty years, the industry has chosen to hit the peg.</p>

<p>The peg is still round.</p>

<p>And the hole is still square.</p>
