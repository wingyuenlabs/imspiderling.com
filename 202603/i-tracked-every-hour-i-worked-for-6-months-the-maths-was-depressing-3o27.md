<p>Freelancing looks great on paper. Pick your hours. Choose your clients. Work from wherever.</p>

<p>Then you do the actual maths.</p>

<h2>
  
  
  The experiment
</h2>

<p>I tracked everything. Not just billable hours — <em>everything</em>. The quoting. The chasing. The admin. The "quick call" that ate 45 minutes. The Saturday morning spent redoing work because the brief changed.</p>

<p>After 6 months, here's what I found:</p>

<ul>
<li>
<strong>Billable hours per week:</strong> 24</li>
<li>
<strong>Total hours worked per week:</strong> 41</li>
<li>
<strong>Effective hourly rate:</strong> 58% of my day rate</li>
</ul>

<p>Nearly half my working time was unpaid.</p>

<p>The payment chasing was the one that stung. Two hours a week, every week, reminding grown adults to pay their invoices. That's over 100 hours a year spent on money I've already earned.</p>

<h2>
  
  
  What actually helped
</h2>

<p>Three things moved the needle:</p>

<p><strong>1. Raise your rate by 40%, not 10%</strong></p>

<p>Most freelancers underprice by at least 30%. Your rate needs to cover the unpaid hours, not just the billable ones. If you're billing 24 hours but working 41, your rate needs to reflect 41 hours of your time.</p>

<p>We built a <a href="https://landolio.com/tools/day-rate-calculator" rel="noopener noreferrer">day rate calculator</a> that factors in the actual numbers. Most people are shocked.</p>

<p><strong>2. Automate the awkward stuff</strong></p>

<p>Payment reminders shouldn't require emotional energy. Set up a sequence: friendly reminder day 1, firmer day 7, formal day 14. Same words every time. Remove yourself from it.</p>

<p>I use templates that escalate automatically. The client gets progressively firmer emails and I don't have to think about tone at 9pm on a Tuesday.</p>

<p><strong>3. Stop quoting for free</strong></p>

<p>Anything over a 30-minute conversation? That's a paid discovery session. Not every prospect will agree. Good. The ones who do are the ones who'll actually pay.</p>

<h2>
  
  
  The uncomfortable truth
</h2>

<p>Your day rate isn't your day rate. Your take-home is your day rate minus all the hours you're not billing for. Until you fix that gap — through pricing, automation, or saying no — you're subsidising your clients with free labour.</p>

<p>Track your hours for a week. All of them. Then do the maths.</p>

<p>You probably won't like the answer. But you'll finally know what to fix.</p>
