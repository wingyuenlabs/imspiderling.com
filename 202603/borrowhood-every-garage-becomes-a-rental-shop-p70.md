<h2>
  
  
  The Community
</h2>

<p>My father Albert was a hand-shovel landscaper in Switzerland from 1960 to 2020. His garage had 500 tools. When he passed, they sat there collecting dust. His neighbors still needed shovels, rakes, and chainsaws -- they just didn't know where to look.</p>

<p>Craigslist is a wasteland. Facebook Marketplace is a data farm. Neither cares about your neighborhood.</p>

<p>BorrowHood is for <strong>real neighborhoods</strong> -- the people on your street who own a drill you need once, a stand mixer sitting idle, a welder gathering dust. Every user is a workshop. Every garage becomes a rental shop. Every kitchen becomes a bakery.</p>

<p>The community I built for: <strong>neighbors who share things instead of buying things they'll use once.</strong></p>

<h2>
  
  
  What I Built
</h2>

<p>A full-stack community rental and sharing platform where people list their underused tools, kitchen gear, equipment, and skills -- and neighbors borrow, rent, or trade. No middlemen. No fees. No algorithm deciding who sees what.</p>

<h3>
  
  
  What it does
</h3>

<p><strong>Eight ways to share:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>
<strong>Rent</strong> -- borrow a drill for the weekend</li>
<li>
<strong>Sell</strong> -- offload gear you don't need</li>
<li>
<strong>Give Away</strong> -- free items, simplified claim flow, earns Generous Neighbor badge</li>
<li>
<strong>Auction</strong> -- competitive bidding with reserve prices and anti-snipe protection</li>
<li>
<strong>Commission</strong> -- custom made-to-order items</li>
<li>
<strong>Service</strong> -- plumbing, tutoring, bike repair</li>
<li>
<strong>Training</strong> -- teach a neighbor your skill</li>
<li>
<strong>Offer</strong> -- name your price / make an offer</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Full rental lifecycle:</strong><br>
Request &gt; Approve &gt; Pickup (lockbox code) &gt; Return (lockbox code) &gt; Review &gt; Complete. With security deposits, dispute resolution, and PayPal payments at every step.</p>

<p><strong>Reputation that means something:</strong><br>
15 badges across 5 tiers (Newcomer to Legend). Reviews are weighted by the reviewer's badge tier -- a Legend's 5-star review counts 10x more than a Newcomer's. Your reputation is earned, not gamed.</p>

<p><strong>Bilingual from day one:</strong><br>
One click switches the entire app between English and Italian. Navigation, forms, badges, categories, filters, error messages -- 500+ translated strings, nothing left behind.</p>

<h3>
  
  
  How Claude Code helped
</h3>

<p>Claude Code (Opus 4.6) was my co-pilot throughout. Not a code generator I copied from -- a pair programmer I worked with.</p>

<p><strong>What worked well:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>
<strong>Scaffolding models and routers</strong> -- describing a feature like "auction system with reserve prices and anti-snipe" and getting a working first draft with the right SQLAlchemy relationships</li>
<li>
<strong>Test generation</strong> -- 250 pytest tests + 52 Puppeteer edge-case screen tests, most written by describing the behavior I wanted tested</li>
<li>
<strong>Edge-case testing</strong> -- Puppeteer tests that probe XSS, pagination boundaries, invalid enums, mobile viewport, broken images -- found a real 500 error on invalid listing type filter</li>
<li>
<strong>i18n coverage</strong> -- translating 476 strings to Italian with context-appropriate translations (not Google Translate quality)</li>
<li>
<strong>Debugging production issues</strong> -- tracing an OAuth redirect loop to a missing port number in an env var, across 4 different config files</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>What I still did myself:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Architecture decisions (Keycloak over simple JWT, server-rendered over SPA)</li>
<li>UI/UX design (every page layout, color choices, the workshop-as-identity concept)</li>
<li>Production deployment and ops (Docker Compose, Caddy config, Hetzner setup)</li>
<li>Manual testing on a real server with real users</li>
</ul>

<p>The commit history tells the story. Every feature was built iteratively -- scaffold, test, deploy, fix, repeat.</p>
