<p>I was going through something on the internet a few days ago and I had to stop to think.</p>

<p>Not another AI tool.</p>

<p>It is not a different productivity app.</p>

<p>And yet a curious and unusual thought.</p>

<p>One of them transformed the developers of GitHub into the buildings within a city.</p>

<p>Of course I had to check it out.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  The First Thing I Saw
</h2>

<p>At the moment the page loaded, I was staring at a skyline made of pixel-art.</p>

<p>Buildings everywhere. Some small. Some massive skyscrapers. Some glowing with lights.</p>

<p>Initially it just appeared to be a game setting.</p>

<p>I then came to think of one interesting thing.</p>

<p>A real GitHub developer was used to represent each building.</p>

<p>And it was not just the size of the buildings.</p>

<p>They were formed from real GitHub activity.</p>

<p>This is when the idea came together.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  Your Code Becomes Architecture
</h2>

<p>The initiative is known as <strong>Git City</strong>.</p>

<p>Rather than presenting your GitHub statistics in the form of charts or graphs, it transforms them into a structure within a virtual city.</p>

<p>What you do on GitHub influences the appearance of your building.</p>

<p>For example:</p>

<ul>
<li>More contributions create <strong>taller buildings</strong>
</li>
<li>More repositories make the building <strong>wider</strong>
</li>
<li>Popular projects can influence <strong>lighting</strong>
</li>
<li>Recent activity can create <strong>glowing effects</strong>
</li>
</ul>

<p>Developers who contribute a lot may appear as <strong>huge skyscrapers in the city</strong>.</p>

<p>Developers who contribute less may appear as <strong>smaller buildings</strong>.</p>

<p>It is a simple concept but surprisingly satisfying to see your coding activity turn into something visual.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  Exploring the City
</h2>

<p>The best part is that you are not just looking at a picture.</p>

<p>You can actually explore the city.</p>

<p>You move around and discover different buildings. Each building represents a developer profile.</p>

<p>When you open one, you can see things like:</p>

<ul>
<li>GitHub stats</li>
<li>repositories</li>
<li>achievements</li>
<li>developer information</li>
</ul>

<p>Instead of scrolling through profile pages, it feels like you are <strong>walking through a city built by developers</strong>.</p>

<p>At one point I found myself just flying around looking for the tallest buildings.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  A Little Bit of Gamification
</h2>

<p>Git City also adds some playful features.</p>

<p>Developers can unlock things like:</p>

<ul>
<li>achievements</li>
<li>building decorations</li>
<li>visual effects</li>
</ul>

<p>These features do not affect GitHub itself. They simply make the city more fun.</p>

<p>It gives a small sense of progression as your building grows with your GitHub activity.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  The City Can Even Show Live Coding Activity
</h2>

<p>There is also a <strong>VS Code extension</strong> connected to the project.</p>

<p>When you are coding, your building can appear active inside the city.</p>

<p>Sometimes it glows or shows activity signals.</p>

<p>It does not share your code. It only sends minimal activity signals.</p>

<p>But it makes the city feel like a place where developers are actually working in real time.</p>

<p>Almost like a <strong>digital city of programmers</strong>.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  The Tech Behind It
</h2>

<p>From a developer perspective, the project is also technically interesting.</p>

<p>The stack includes:</p>

<ul>
<li>Next.js</li>
<li>React</li>
<li>React Three Fiber with Three.js</li>
<li>TypeScript</li>
<li>Supabase</li>
</ul>

<p>Three.js allows the platform to render the city and keep everything interactive.</p>

<p>It is a good example of how modern web technologies can create experiences that feel almost like small games inside the browser.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  A Quick Reality Check
</h2>

<p>GitHub activity does not always equal skill.</p>

<p>Some developers commit frequently. Others contribute less often but build very impactful projects.</p>

<p>So this visualization should be seen as something <strong>fun and experimental</strong>, not a ranking system.</p>

<h2>
  
  
  Final Thoughts
</h2>

<p>GitHub has always been full of numbers. Commits, repositories, contribution graphs.</p>

<p>Git City turns those numbers into something visual.</p>

<p>A skyline built from developer activity.</p>

<p>And when you explore it, you realize something interesting.</p>

<p>Every building in that city represents someone somewhere writing code.</p>
