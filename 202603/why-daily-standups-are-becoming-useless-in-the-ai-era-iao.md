Title: Why Daily Standups Are Becoming Useless in the AI Era

<p>Daily standups were supposed to improve coordination.</p>

<p>In practice, they often became a ritual that burns engineering time without giving much back. The old 15-minute promise sounds harmless, but in most real teams it becomes 30 minutes, 1 hour, or even 1 hour 30 minutes once the conversation starts and people wait their turn.</p>

<p>Strictly speaking, "daily" is the cadence and "standup" is the ceremony. In practice, people just use "daily" as shorthand for the meeting itself.</p>

<p>That is where the math gets ugly.</p>

<h2>
  
  
  Why it used to make sense
</h2>

<p>Standups were useful when teams had poor visibility and weak async tooling. They helped surface blockers, expose dependencies, and give managers a quick snapshot of progress.</p>

<p>The problem is that many companies kept the ceremony long after the reason for it weakened.</p>

<p>Today, engineers already have:</p>

<ul>
<li>ticket boards</li>
<li>pull request summaries</li>
<li>commit history</li>
<li>Slack updates</li>
<li>AI-generated status recaps</li>
</ul>

<p>If the status already exists in the system of record, forcing the whole team into a synchronous update ritual is usually redundant.</p>

<h2>
  
  
  The real cost
</h2>

<p>The meeting itself is not the full cost. The expensive part is the context switching before and after it.</p>

<p>Let’s make the assumptions explicit:</p>

<ul>
<li>5 workdays per week</li>
<li>one daily standup every workday</li>
<li>everyone attends</li>
<li>the realistic range is 30 minutes to 1 hour 30 minutes per day</li>
</ul>

<h3>
  
  
  Cost per engineer
</h3>

<p>If the daily lasts <strong>30 minutes</strong>:</p>

<ul>
<li>per week: <code>2.5 hours</code>
</li>
<li>per month: <code>10.8 hours</code>
</li>
<li>per year: <code>130 hours</code>
</li>
</ul>

<p>If the daily lasts <strong>1 hour 30 minutes</strong>:</p>

<ul>
<li>per week: <code>7.5 hours</code>
</li>
<li>per month: <code>32.5 hours</code>
</li>
<li>per year: <code>390 hours</code>
</li>
</ul>

<p>A single engineer can lose <strong>130 to 390 hours per year</strong> to a ritual that is supposed to save time.</p>

<h2>
  
  
  Visualizing the waste
</h2>

<p>The charts make the hidden tax easier to see.</p>

<h3>
  
  
  6-person startup
</h3>

<p>If the team has 6 people:</p>

<ul>
<li>
<strong>30 minutes</strong> per day = <code>780 hours</code> per year</li>
<li>
<strong>1 hour 30 minutes</strong> per day = <code>2,340 hours</code> per year</li>
</ul>

<p>That is roughly <strong>0.4 to 1.1 full-time work-years</strong> every year.</p>

<h3>
  
  
  300-person company
</h3>

<p>If the company has 300 people:</p>

<ul>
<li>
<strong>30 minutes</strong> per day = <code>39,000 hours</code> per year</li>
<li>
<strong>1 hour 30 minutes</strong> per day = <code>117,000 hours</code> per year</li>
</ul>

<p>That is roughly <strong>18.8 to 56.3 full-time work-years</strong> every year.</p>

<h2>
  
  
  What AI changed
</h2>

<p>AI did not remove the need for communication. It removed a lot of the manual work that used to justify the meeting.</p>

<p>Teams can now get:</p>

<ul>
<li>better summaries</li>
<li>faster blocker detection</li>
<li>cleaner async updates</li>
<li>more visible project state</li>
</ul>

<p>So the daily often becomes a low-value repetition of information people already have.</p>

<h2>
  
  
  What should replace it
</h2>

<p>Not silence. Better communication.</p>

<p>For most teams, that means:</p>

<ul>
<li>async updates in Slack, Linear, Jira, or Notion</li>
<li>clear ownership</li>
<li>visible blockers</li>
<li>short syncs only when a real decision or dependency needs conversation</li>
</ul>

<p>If the team still needs a live meeting, it should be because it is solving something that cannot be solved async.</p>

<p>Reading yesterday’s task list is not that.</p>

<h2>
  
  
  The rule I would use
</h2>

<ul>
<li>keep a daily only if it consistently removes blockers faster than async communication</li>
<li>otherwise, replace it with written updates or a few syncs per week</li>
<li>reserve live meetings for decisions, incidents, and real collaboration</li>
</ul>

<h2>
  
  
  Bottom line
</h2>

<p>Daily standups made sense when information was hard to see.</p>

<p>In the AI era, they are often just a recurring tax on engineering time.</p>

<p>If the meeting cannot prove its value, it should not survive by habit.</p>
