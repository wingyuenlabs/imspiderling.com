<h1>
  
  
  Communication Is Important — But It’s Not the Most Important Thing in Engineering Teams
</h1>

<p>“Communication is key.”</p>

<p>We hear this all the time.</p>

<p>And yes — communication matters.</p>

<p>But here’s the uncomfortable truth:</p>

<p><strong>Too much communication is often a sign of broken processes.</strong></p>




<h2>
  
  
  Not everything should depend on talking
</h2>

<p>In many teams, people say:</p>

<ul>
<li>“Let’s align on this”
</li>
<li>“We should discuss this”
</li>
<li>“Can we jump on a quick call?”
</li>
</ul>

<p>But why does everything need alignment?</p>

<p>Why do we need constant clarification?</p>

<p>As product leader Marty Cagan often emphasizes, strong teams are empowered by <strong>clear context and autonomy</strong> — not constant coordination.</p>

<p>If people need to ask at every step, something deeper is missing.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  The real problem: lack of clarity
</h2>

<p>The biggest issue is not communication.</p>

<p>It’s this question:</p>

<blockquote>
<p>“Where does my responsibility start and end?”</p>
</blockquote>

<p>When that’s not clear:</p>

<ul>
<li>People overstep
</li>
<li>Or worse — no one takes ownership
</li>
<li>Decisions get delayed
</li>
<li>Meetings multiply
</li>
</ul>

<p>This connects directly with a classic idea from Melvin Conway:</p>

<p><strong>Systems mirror the communication structure of organizations.</strong></p>

<p>If your system is full of dependencies, your team will rely on constant communication just to function.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  When communication becomes a crutch
</h2>

<p>Without strong processes and culture:</p>

<ul>
<li>Communication turns into dependency
</li>
<li>Teams rely on conversations instead of structure
</li>
<li>Knowledge stays in people’s heads, not in the system
</li>
</ul>

<p>Engineering leader Camille Fournier highlights this well:</p>

<p>Teams that depend too much on synchronous communication usually lack strong documentation and clear processes.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  The deeper issue: unclear thinking
</h2>

<p>There’s another layer here.</p>

<p>As Sam Altman has pointed out:</p>

<p><strong>Unclear communication is often a symptom of unclear thinking.</strong></p>

<p>If the system, the problem, or the goal isn’t well understood, no amount of communication will fix it.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  What actually makes a team agile
</h2>

<p>Agility is not:</p>

<ul>
<li>More meetings
</li>
<li>Faster replies
</li>
<li>Constant alignment
</li>
</ul>

<p>Even decades ago, management thinker Peter Drucker argued that meetings are often a symptom of poor structure.</p>

<p>Real agility comes from:</p>

<ul>
<li>Clear responsibilities
</li>
<li>Well-defined processes
</li>
<li>Autonomy with boundaries
</li>
<li>Shared understanding of how work flows
</li>
</ul>

<p>Communication supports this.</p>

<p>It does not replace it.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  A more mature way to think
</h2>

<p>Instead of asking:</p>

<blockquote>
<p>“Did we communicate enough?”</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Start asking:</p>

<blockquote>
<p>“Was this supposed to require communication at all?”</p>
</blockquote>




<h2>
  
  
  Final thought
</h2>

<p>Great teams don’t communicate more.</p>

<p>They communicate <strong>better and less</strong> — because the system already does most of the work.</p>

<p>And without structure, no amount of communication will make a team truly agile.</p>
