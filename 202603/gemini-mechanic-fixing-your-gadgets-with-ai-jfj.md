<h2>
  
  
  What I Built with Google Gemini
</h2>

<p>I created a project called <strong>Gemini Mechanic</strong>. It is a smart assistant that helps people fix broken hardware and electronics. Many people feel lost when a gadget stops working. My web app lets users take a photo of the problem or type a description to get help right away.</p>

<p>Google Gemini is the brain of this project. It looks at the photos to figure out what is wrong. Then it gives the user a list of repair steps and tells them what parts they need to buy. This makes it much easier for regular people to repair their own things.</p>

<h2>
  
  
  What I Learned
</h2>

<p>One of the biggest lessons I learned was about mobile design. Most people are not at a desk when they are trying to fix something. They are usually holding their phone while they work. I spent a lot of time making the app mobile responsive so it looks good on small screens.</p>

<p>I also learned how to write better instructions for the AI. For repairs, safety is the most important part. I had to make sure Gemini always reminds users to do things like disconnect the battery before they start working.</p>




<h2>
  
  
  Google Gemini Feedback
</h2>

<p>Working with Gemini was a good experience, but I did face some challenges:</p>

<ul>
<li><p><strong>Picking the right model:</strong> I spent a lot of time testing different Gemini models. I needed one that was smart enough to recognize small parts but fast enough to work on a phone. I don’t know why whenever I try to use Gemini as the ai for any of my projects, I always run into errors. But with the project I was pushed to figure it out, took me a couple of hours till I found the best model to run it with. </p></li>
<li><p><strong>Mobile speed:</strong> Making sure the app stayed fast while the AI was thinking was tricky. I had to optimize the code so the user experience stayed smooth on mobile devices.</p></li>
</ul>
