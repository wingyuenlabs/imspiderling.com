<h2>
  
  
  The Forgetting Problem
</h2>

<p>Your brain isn't built to retain six months of technical work. You shipped a critical performance fix in March. You architected a new service in July. You onboarded a new team member in September. These are significant accomplishments, but by December, they're buried under dozens of other commits, merged PRs, and resolved issues.</p>

<p><a href="https://www.bragdoc.ai/why-it-matters" rel="noopener noreferrer">Performance reviews</a> force you into a scramble. You're scrolling through your GitHub history at 11 PM the night before your review, trying to piece together narratives from git logs you barely remember contributing to. You miss important context. You downplay significant work because the details have faded. You stress because you know you've forgotten substantial contributions.</p>

<p>This is worse than forgetting a task at the grocery store. Your career advancement depends on documenting your work clearly and completely.</p>

<h2>
  
  
  Why Manual Brag Documents Fail
</h2>

<p>Manual brag doc systems—whether they're Notion templates, Google Docs, Markdown files, or specialized tracking apps—share the same fatal flaw: they require discipline you don't have. <a href="https://www.bragdoc.ai/pricing" rel="noopener noreferrer">See our pricing page</a> for a better solution.</p>

<p>The theory is simple: update your brag doc consistently throughout the year. By review time, you have a comprehensive record.</p>

<p>The reality is different. You're heads-down in code. You forget to document that refactor because you were focused on solving the problem. You skip the weekly update because you had a deadline. You tell yourself you'll catch up later. By the time review season arrives, your manual doc contains maybe 20% of your actual work.</p>

<p>Here's the core problem: <strong>manual documentation adds friction</strong>. It requires you to context-switch, remember what you did, articulate it clearly, and manually enter it into a system. That's cognitive overhead on top of your actual job. Most developers don't do it consistently.</p>

<p>You can read about how discipline is the answer, but that ignores how human brains work. Relying on willpower for documentation is a losing strategy.</p>

<h2>
  
  
  Git History Is Your Perfect Source of Truth
</h2>

<p>Your Git history is already there. It's already complete. It's already detailed.</p>

<p>Every commit you make is automatically timestamped and documented. The commit message explains why you made the change. And if it didn’t, the diff shows exactly what you changed and how. Your GitHub profile tracks when these contributions happened. Pull request discussions capture context and decision-making.</p>

<p>This is an objective record that requires zero additional effort. You're already creating this documentation every time you commit code.</p>

<p>Compare this to manual brag docs, where you're relying on memory, judgment, and motivation to write something down. Git history is objectively better as a data source.</p>
